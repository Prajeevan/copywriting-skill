# Copywriting

> "Copywriting isn't just writing words. It's understanding what people want and how to get them to do what you want them to do. Then figuring out which words are needed to get the desired outcome." — Sam Parr

The job of copy is **behavior change**, not decoration. You are getting what's in the reader's head aligned with what's in yours, and moving them to act. Words are the last 20% of the work.

## Core philosophy (read this first)

1. **The goal is NOT to get 100% of people to engage. It's to get the RIGHT person to read all of it — and a large percentage of them will buy.** Stop optimizing for everyone. Optimize for the qualified reader finishing the whole thing.
2. **"There is no such thing as too long, only too boring."** Long copy almost always out-converts short copy. Ugly almost always out-converts pretty. The only real failure is *uninteresting*. When tempted to cut for length, cut for *boredom* instead.
3. **Niches make riches.** Copy that tries to speak to everyone speaks to no one. Narrow targeting → higher engagement, even though it "excludes" people.
4. **Constraint forces clarity.** The discipline of writing short, simple, story-driven copy is what makes it land.

When a user says "no one will read this, it's too long" — push back: it's not too long, it's not interesting *enough*. Fix the interest, not the length.

## The workflow: how to actually produce copy

Follow this order. Do not skip to clever wordsmithing.

1. **Identify the one reader.** Who exactly is this for? Get specific enough to write *to one person* ("if you make over $3M/year, I know exactly how you feel"). Name their problem, their secret frustration, what they've already tried and failed at.
2. **Pick the structure** (AIDA or a story arc — see below).
3. **Write the hook/headline first**, not last. (See "Headlines.") Spend disproportionate effort here.
4. **Write the body as a slippery slope** — every sentence exists to get the next one read.
5. **Write drunk, edit sober.** Vomit the first draft — "you've got to write the crap, just get it on paper." Ogilvy: *"I'm a lousy writer, but a good editor."*
6. **Cut a third. Then cut a third again.** ("Kill your darlings" / "write with your eraser.") This is where 80% of the quality comes from — it forces out the actual point.
7. **Read it for rhythm and reading level** (see "Mechanics").
8. **Layer in proof, objection-handling, and the CTA.**

When delivering copy to a user, it's often worth showing the hook options (3–5 headline variants) and explaining the *framework* behind your choices so they can steer.

## Finding the idea (what to even write about)

Most "I can't write" is really "I don't know what to say." Before wordsmithing, find an idea you already know will land. Sam: *"There's no such thing as 'I just want to get into content' — that's like asking 'how do you make art?' Get more specific."*

**Three buckets of content** — know which one you're in:
1. **Search-intent** — what people are already Googling ("Mailchimp vs ConvertKit"). You want to rank #1 and convert. Straightforward but not viral.
2. **Opinion** — "things I think the world should hear." Powerful, but **needs a platform first**: "If 21-year-old Oprah said 'my opinion is X,' you'd say 'who are you?'" Earn the right.
3. **Deadline-driven** — you must publish something that gets read, with a business objective behind it. This is where the mining methods below earn their keep.

**Mine where people already reveal what they care about.** Don't invent topics; harvest them.
- **Reddit, sorted by Top.** There's a subreddit for everything, and it ranks content by what people actually upvote. Read the post — but **the gold is in the comments.** (Keto subreddit → top comment "the answer to everything here is electrolytes" → article idea. Home-gym subreddit → 2nd comment "is this equipment rust-proof?" → "they just told you what to write.") A **fast-growing subreddit** is an emerging trend (DIY soap).
- **Paste a URL into the places your people hang out** (Hacker News, relevant subreddits, Facebook groups) and read the *sentiment* in the replies. (Paul Graham's "Mean People Fail" essay → the pushback thread → Sam's angle: *"Do nice guys really finish last? Here are 10 jerks who crushed it."*)
- **News comment sections** are "the cesspool of the internet" and a goldmine. (Ken Griffin moving his fund Chicago→Florida; top comment blames liberal-run cities → angle: *"Do liberal cities actually have more crime?"*)

**The "one line" method (the big idea / hook).** Out of a 40–50 minute talk or podcast, you're hunting for the **single sentence that made you go "wait, WHAT?"** — then you write the whole piece around just that. *"I'll sit through 40 minutes of boring, then they say one line and I go: that's the article."* (Pandora's founder, minute ~46 of a talk: *"our employees worked for free for two years"* → Sam's all-time first viral hit. Interviewing a franchise builder who offhandedly says builds take 3 years "because there are so few carpenters" → "Why America has so few carpenters," hundreds of thousands of reads.) You can do this just browsing Twitter/YouTube/podcasts — scan for the one line worth expanding.

**Quantity vs. quality is a false dichotomy.** *"Dog, let's do both — write good stuff and a lot of it."* Early on, lean quantity to find what resonates; you get instantaneous feedback on social and can see fast what's compelling.

## Headlines & hooks — where 80% of the value is

> Ogilvy: "On average, five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy. When you've written your headline, you've spent 80 cents of your dollar."

Ogilvy reportedly spent *weeks* writing the single Rolls-Royce headline: *"At 60 mph the loudest noise in this new Rolls-Royce comes from the electric clock."* Treat the headline as the majority of the job.

**The first sentence must punch you in the face.** If the first line doesn't grab, nothing else matters.
- Sam's classic: *"You're reading this not because you want to, but because I want you to."* (then) *"Now you're reading this second sentence because, again, I'm telling you and forcing you to read this."* — breaks the fourth wall, demonstrates control, creates a slippery slope.
- The Hustle's best-ever ad (responsible for the exit): *"My friends think I'm smart. I'm not."* Curiosity + relatability in two short sentences.

### The Curiosity Gap ("mind the gap" / open loops)
Humans cannot leave a cliffhanger unresolved — like an unresolved musical note, it creates tension that *demands* release. Open a loop the reader must keep reading to close.
- Classic: *"They laughed when I sat down at the piano — but then I started to play."* (Hopkins-era ad selling a music course.)
- Upworthy-style: *"...you won't believe number 7."*
- In **short-form video**, stack open loops — ideally a new curiosity gap roughly **every ~8 seconds**, and especially right before predicted drop-off points (watch the retention graph; where people leave, insert a new gap).
- Tony Robbins does this across whole books: "in this chapter I'll tell you X, but wait until the next chapter — that's when something will blow your mind."

### The headline is a spoken sentence
Sam's #1 headline move: **make the headline the one sentence you'd say to a friend at a bar.** Imagine you just heard a 60-minute Buffett podcast and remembered one line — the headline is *"Here's how Warren Buffett made 99% of his wealth after age 60."* He usually starts with the headline, then writes the piece toward it (it may shift as he works — "more art than science"). Constant question: *"How would I explain this in literally one sentence to someone over dinner?"*

**The sub-headline continues the conversation — it doesn't repeat the headline.** Headline opens the loop; sub-headline (meta description / the line under it on a social share) keeps the story sliding. *Headline:* "Buffett made 99% of his net worth after 60." *Sub-head:* "The secret? Patience. In this podcast he goes deep on exactly how long it takes." Order of importance: **headline first, picture second, sub-headline third.**

**The picture is co-equal copy.** "A picture's worth a thousand words" — wrong images quietly kill great copy; a video beats almost anything. Before/after photos are catchy even when the two images barely differ. Treat the thumbnail/hero with the same seriousness as the headline. (Felix Dennis / Jann Wenner rule for magazines: spend as much on the cover as on everything else combined — *"people judge a book by its cover."*)

**Write a different headline for each channel.** Social vs. search want different things. Soylent piece — *social:* "What happened when I went 30 days without food"; *search:* "Soylent: How I went 30 days without food" (because people Google "Soylent," so lead with the keyword).

### Emotion drives sharing — pick the right one
What gets shared is governed by the emotion it triggers. **Awe and anger share best. Curiosity is strong. Sadness shares worst** — depressing content gets the TV turned off (those Sarah McLachlan ASPCA ads). Choose the emotion *before* you write the headline.
- **Awe** (the Zack Crockett school): specific, almost-unbelievable true stories. "The man who won the lottery 14 times." "The man feeding a remote Alaska town with a Costco card and a ship." "Why America has so few carpenters." "The lucrative economics of [X]." You finish reading and think *how is that real?*
- **Anger / contrarian — done ethically.** Pick a defensible take that makes a chunk of readers want to argue. "Why buying a house is a bad financial decision." "Losers exist. Don't hire them." The goal is *"you're probably ready to punch me — good, now you'll read it,"* **not** rage-bait or fake news. Have a real argument; don't break integrity for clicks.

### Headline patterns that work
- **Targeting / "niches make riches":** "50 reasons you know you're from Denver." Only Denver people care — but 30% of them click. (BuzzFeed model; Homesick Candles = a candle per state; Rolex "the men who shape destinies wear Rolex.") Speak so intimately to a narrow group that *they* feel seen.
- **Twist a known phrase 3%:** Take a famous phrase/idea and bend it to your brand. "You may get lost, but not in the crowd." "Pick up 9× more women in this than in a Lamborghini." Borrow rhetorical structures (e.g., "The only thing we have to fear is fear itself"; Churchill's "we shall fight on the beaches" repetition) for memorability — alliteration and parallel syntax matter.
- **Make the abstract violently concrete/visual:** Don't say "150g of fat" — say "imagine a brick of fat this big." Don't say "non-toxic storage" — say **"Imagine storing your food in the toilet bowl. Because that's basically what you're doing."** Reframe the boring claim against a vivid, almost shocking image.
- **Curiosity + payoff structure:** specific setup → gap → resolution.

## Body copy: the Slippery Slope

> Joe Sugarman: "The sole purpose of the first sentence is to get you to read the second sentence. The purpose of the second is to get you to read the third." And so on.

- **Rule of consistency / micro-commitments:** Small yeses lead to big yeses. (Foot-in-the-door: agree to the small sign → ~40% more likely to agree to the big one.) In copy, get the reader nodding "yes" as early as possible — ask a question they must answer yes to: *"Don't you notice that suddenly after 35 you wake up at 2–3am and can't fall back asleep?"* → mental yes → they're sliding.
- **The Trojan Horse:** Bury the product deep inside the story. Build desire for a long time *before* revealing what you're selling. Bad ads throw the product at you in sentence one ("our daily restorative drink"); great ads make you *want* the outcome first, then reveal the product as the inevitable solution.

## Storytelling — "story, story, story, story"

People resist stories in sales copy because they fear length. Wrong fear. **"There's no such thing as too long, just too boring — and the best way to make something interesting is to make it a story."**

- **Every story = beginning, middle, end + tension.**
- **Hero's Journey, positioned correctly:** the **customer is the hero**; your **brand is the guide** (the mentor, not the star). E.g., Wall Street Journal "Two Young Men" ad (ran 28 years, ~$2B in revenue): two identical graduates, one subscribed to the WSJ, that one became far more successful. The reader is the boy; the product is what guides him to the light. Include the "return to origin" beat where the hero comes back changed.
- **"But / therefore," never "and then, and then."** (Trey Parker / South Park rule.) Stories advance on conflict and consequence: "He was about to make life-changing money, **but** he was in a room with 14 lawyers and felt totally overwhelmed, **therefore** he needed a group of peers..." Strings of "and then" are boring; "but/therefore" creates momentum.
- **Failed-solution / absolve the guilt:** Tell the reader their past failure isn't their fault — they just lacked the right mechanism. "You tried to fix your stress and it didn't work. That's not your fault. You just didn't have the right ingredient." This lowers defenses and sets up your product as the missing piece.
- **Framebreaking content** travels: show something that violates the audience's assumption of how the world works (Sam's "here's how my dad sells $10M/yr of onions from a desk with two phones and a calculator" — viral because it breaks the frame of what a big business "should" look like).
- **The story premium is real.** Companies that get valued beyond their numbers usually have a story behind them — "an incredible mission is just another way to say story" (Tesla, Palantir "securing democracy," Anduril, a founder who "looks a little strange and says wacky things"). Whatever you're selling, the *why you made it* often matters more than the what. Intent reads as authenticity; authenticity is what spreads.

## The educational ad (worked AIDA example)

**AIDA = Attention, Interest, Desire, Action.** A reliable template for an educational/story ad:

- **Attention:** A surprising, specific, targeted stat or claim. *"There's a scientific reason why after age 35, 75% of men wake up between 2 and 3am."*
- **Interest:** Describe their lived experience back to them so precisely they think *"that's me."* "You know the feeling — you wake up, not sure if you need to pee or what's going on, and for years you just assumed it was age."
- **Desire:** Reveal the real cause + that it's fixable + it's not their fault. "The truth is it's because you lack vitamin D... the good news is it's easy to fix." Then introduce the product as the superior version ("most vitamin D isn't absorbed — ours is; we spent 10 years and $100M sourcing it").
- **Action:** Clear CTA + social proof. *"Trusted by 100,000 dads just like you."*

**Same model, newer vocabulary:** what Instagram/TikTok creators call **"hook, meat, CTA"** is exactly AIDA — the hook is the first ~5 sentences that grab attention, the meat builds interest + desire, the CTA is the action. The deep aim throughout: write so intimately the reader thinks *"this person knows my problem better than I do — they must have the solution."* (Weight-loss example, said *to her*: "I know why you offer to take the photo. I know why you wear the cover-up. I know why you stand at the edge of the group." Use this power for good.)

(Note from Sam: the specific facts above are invented for illustration — when you write a real ad, get the *actual* stats. Use research/Claude to find true, vivid, specific numbers. Never fabricate claims in real copy.)

## Handling objections

Pre-empt the reader's doubts *before* they voice them — the **8 Mile move**: say the bad thing about yourself first so the objection has no power left. ("I know what you're thinking — who is this person, why should I trust them?")
- **Personal anecdote beats a spec claim.** Don't claim "waterproof" — say *"I accidentally wore them in the shower and they were totally fine."* Don't claim "great battery" — say *"I haven't charged them in 3 weeks."* Anecdotes dissolve objections without triggering "yeah, sure" skepticism.
- Park objections in an **FAQ** section if they don't fit the flow.
- Address the price objection by reframing value ("you're buying an experience, and experiences make you happier than things — and this one happens to save you money too").

## Proof

Often the single best ad is just **proof**: a before/after, a screenshot of real results (e.g., a sleep-tracker score before vs. after), a customer quote. For a transformation product, show the transformation and *don't even show the product*. Layer **social proof** ("trusted by 100,000 dads like you," covered by Forbes/Inc., etc.).

## Landing pages & product copy

- **Above the fold should be a feeling, not a feature.** Lead with the emotion you remove or deliver, not your feature list. Tax software: don't headline "AI tax assistant for professionals" — headline the dread: *"You know that feeling when your accountant messages you and you owe a huge bill you never saw coming? No more."* Taking the terror out of tax season.
- **Avoid the "feature death march."** When you're too close to the product you pitch the last three features you shipped instead of the big vision. Test your lines like a comedian in small rooms — say them to 10–20 real customers and watch which one makes them *lean in*; that line goes at the top.
- **Long pages convert** — Amazon and Apple product pages are enormous (photos + thousands of words + reviews) for a reason: skimmers need multiple attention-grabs to pull them back onto the slippery slope. *"It's not that long beats short — it's that you want the fewest words that are maximally effective, and that's often a lot of words."*
- **A letter from the founder beats a slick grid.** For a smallish company, a plain personal letter ("Hey there, fellow accountant — let me explain. I used to be one, and I felt this every day…") outperforms fancy graphics. Sam's bet: *"I could sell more with a Google Doc of plain text than most companies do with a page full of pretty photos."*
- **The About page is usually the 2nd-most-visited page — so tell a story there, and pull it onto the homepage.** (Hampton's About page leads with co-founder Joe's *Inc.*-documented $100M loss when a Facebook algorithm change wiped out his company right before sale — that story pulls you in.)

## The "forgotten text" — novelty hides in the defaults

A reliable edge: rewrite the copy nobody bothers with because it ships as a template. Welcome emails, signup pop-ups, "follow us" lines, unsubscribe text, confirmation pages, empty states, the 404. Default text is a wasted slot — make it cute, personal, and **frame-breaking** and people will screenshot it.
- Sam's long-form **welcome email** ("a letter from the leader of the circus") and his deliberately *long* signup pop-up both got written up by other blogs — because everyone "knows" pop-ups should be short, so a long one breaks the frame. The Soylent pop-up converted ~3%: *"Oh my god, not another pop-up. Okay — the pop-up's already here and so are you, so give me a second… we're a daily newsletter, we make crazy stuff like the article you're reading. Hate it? Email me and I'll Venmo you a dollar."*
- (Louis CK's checkout/signup copy is the canonical example of personality in "forgotten" UI text.)

## Using AI in copywriting

LLMs can't write the final copy — internet-native readers spot "this was written by AI, and it sounds dumb." People with personality still win. But AI is a great **sparring partner** for the parts that aren't the prose:
- **Find the big idea / fresh angle.** Ask for many one-liners and analogies, throw out 95%, keep the one spark. (Sam took "you're the average of your 5 friends," asked for harder evidence, and got a 1976 study tracking 20,000 people on obesity/poverty/happiness — turning a tired phrase into a science-backed hook.)
- **Find true, vivid, specific stats** to make claims concrete (then verify — never ship fabricated numbers).
- **Channel a voice.** Scrape a writer you love (e.g. Scott Galloway), ask AI to rewrite your draft in their style — the output is mostly unusable, but it occasionally re-sequences a sentence or surfaces an analogy worth stealing. *You* still make every final call and rewrite in your own voice.
- Grammarly/Hemingway help on the margins (passive voice, adverbs, reading level) — ideas still matter most.

## Mechanics: rhythm, reading level, length

- **Rhythm is everything.** Vary sentence length: short, short, medium, long, short. "Good writing is like music — it starts, it stops." Don't ramble in same-length sentences. Some sentences are short. Some are a little longer. And occasionally one runs long because it's carrying the reader somewhere and building toward what comes next.
- **Start sentences with "And" or "But."** Transition words create rhythm and pull the eye down. Use them freely.
- **One sentence = one point.** Where you'd use a comma, often use a period instead.
- **No em-dashes. No semicolons.** In the copy you ship, never use em-dashes (—) or semicolons (;). They read as stiff, formal, or AI-written, and they let you cram two thoughts into one breath when you should just stop. Use a period and start a new sentence. A plain comma or parentheses is fine when you truly need a quick aside. (This rule is about *output copy*; the dashes in this reference doc are not the deliverable.)
- **Use the active voice.** "The boy kicked the ball," never "the ball was kicked by the boy." Someone should be *doing* something, not having it done to them. Most people default to the weaker passive — catch it.
- **Kill adverbs.** Stephen King: *"The road to hell is paved with adverbs."* Cut "really," "very," "actually," "quickly."
- **The thesaurus test:** *if you have to look a word up, don't use it.* Writing complicated is easy; writing simple is hard — and simplicity *is* the service you're providing. Don't mistake big words for intelligence.
- **Write at a low reading level — aim ~4th–7th grade.** (USA Today ≈ 4th grade, NYT ≈ 7th grade.) Run copy through hemingwayapp.com; if it scores 12th grade, that's a problem, not a brag. Warren Buffett explains incredibly complex businesses (insurance!) at roughly **16–18 words per sentence** — and his average has dropped steadily as he's aged ("the tide goes out and you see who's swimming naked"). Hemingway conveyed profound emotion in plain words ("the man was sad, he wanted to go fish"). Simple ≠ shallow.
- **Cut a third, then cut a third again.** Repeat. This is where the point emerges.

## Other levers

- **Humor:** "You can say virtually anything as long as you make them laugh" (true in sales and flirting). Works best when it's genuinely part of the brand voice. The New York Post headlines are the gold standard ("SoFi → So [expletive]"). Don't force it on brands where it doesn't fit.
- **Time constraint / takeaway:** Imposing a limit makes people lean in. "I've only got a minute, but quickly —" or "what I'm about to tell you will blow your mind, but real quick let me say this first." Creates urgency and permission to be brief, paradoxically increasing attention.
- **Restaurant-owner energy:** Talk to the reader like you already know them. Warm, familiar, presumptive — not stiff. Stiffness reads as "what's wrong with this person?"
- **Bottom-of-funnel is allowed to be direct.** High-intent audiences (e.g. someone already pre-qualifying for financing) don't always need the long desire build — but you can still wrap price inside desire rather than leading with it.
- **Inject your personal life into "all business" copy** — especially B2B. A dry affiliate-marketing newsletter opens warmer with: *"Writing this from Brooklyn for my sister's wedding — walked into a bodega and found a CBD drink, never see those in Boise, let's see if it does anything. Anyway, today's email…"* Founders sharing "the 18 books that shaped me" or an odd hobby (Sam collects vintage denim) makes people *like* you, which makes them buy.

## Distribution: great copy still has to be carried

The best ad nobody sees does nothing. *"Everyone's over there; you're over here, a nobody. Siphon them off to you."* Go where the audience already is (relevant subreddits, Facebook groups, Hacker News, Twitter), add real value, and link back — rather than writing into the void on your own blog. Sam grew The Hustle to ~100k subscribers mostly by posting story-driven pieces into big subreddits when his own list was 50 people. **It's a grind:** run a checklist every post (submitted to these subreddits, posted in these groups, emailed the list, asked 5–10 friends to share), and accept that most attempts get ~10 readers while a few spike — and each spike plateaus you a little higher. Eventually you're big enough that people repost you for free.

## Copy Work — how to actually get good (the #1 practice)

To develop taste in any craft you imitate the masters until you internalize the rules — same as learning piano by copying "Jingle Bells," then harder songs, until you can blend influences into your own style.

**Copy Work = find writing that's regarded as great, and copy it out by hand, word for word, for an hour a day ("copy hour"), for 3–6 months.**
- **Do it by hand — 100%, not typing.** There's a muscular/neurological link: handwriting inscribes the patterns deeper in memory (it consolidates while you sleep); typing barely moves the hand and doesn't stick. Cursive on lined paper is even better. *"Writing is clarity of thought — a clear writer is always a clear thinker"* (it's why Bezos mandates narrative memos over slides). This is the single easiest way to get better, and almost nobody does it.
- Start with great **ads** and **sales pages** (Google "best ads of all time," "highest-converting sales letters," The Boron Letters; hey.com is a great modern sales page), then branch into the genre you want: short stories (*Catcher in the Rye*, Steinbeck), SNL skits and comedy scripts (for timing/humor), famous speeches, novels you love (Gatsby, Hemingway, a Jack Reacher).
- As you copy, notice the *rules*: where they place a transition word, where they open a curiosity gap, how they pace tension. Then steal a bit from each and combine into your own voice.
- **Use it as a warm-up.** Right before you write something important, spend 5–10 minutes hand-copying someone whose voice you want to channel — "motion creates emotion," like running scales before you play. You get in the groove, *then* write your own thing. If you're blocked, copy for 20 minutes and you'll almost always come out with something to say.
- This same method works for any craft (recreate a beat, a website in Canva, a video shot).

**Keep a swipe file.** Steal relentlessly and "twist it 3%." Sam pulls ideas from Thrasher, Japanese magazines (Popeye), vintage architecture mags, old ads — anywhere outside his own industry, so the borrowed ideas feel fresh in context. ("If you ever see me write about copywriting, I'm really just stealing from Ogilvy/Hopkins/Sugarman.") A swipe file of your favorite ads/books also makes a great lead magnet.

## Joseph Sugarman's system — the engine under all of this

Sam's playbook is largely Sugarman applied. *The Adweek Copywriting Handbook* is the source of the slippery slope and a complete persuasion system worth knowing directly. (Sugarman wrote the legendary BluBlocker and JS&A mail-order ads — long copy that sold from a page of plain text.)

### Sugarman's axioms (the load-bearing ones, in his words)
*(He numbers ~17 axioms; the wording below is his, but treat exact axiom numbers as approximate — the concepts are what matter.)*
- **Axiom 2 — "All the elements in an advertisement are designed to do one thing: get you to read the first sentence."** The headline, picture, layout, subhead — their *only* job is to earn the first line.
- **Axiom 3 — "The sole purpose of the first sentence is to get you to read the second sentence"** (and the second's, the third). **Make the first sentence almost impossible *not* to finish** — so short and easy it's read before the reader decides whether to. ("Losing weight isn't easy." "Cigars stink.")
- **Axiom 4 — "Your ad layout and the first few paragraphs must create the buying environment most conducive to the sale."** Before you persuade, set the *atmosphere* — tone, imagery, and word choice that pre-dispose the reader to your product. Every product wants its own environment (luxury watch ≠ discount tool).
- **Axiom 6 — The Slippery Slide:** the reader should be so caught they "cannot stop reading until they've read all of it, as if sliding down a greased chute." Remove every bit of friction; build momentum.
- **Resonance / "the buyer and seller must vibrate together":** make true, agreeable statements early so the reader keeps nodding *yes*. The moment they disagree or disbelieve, harmony breaks and they leave. (Same mechanism as Sam's "rule of consistency / micro-yeses.")
- **"Never sell a product — always sell a concept."** Don't sell a thermostat; sell "set it and forget it." Don't sell features; sell the *idea*. And his most-quoted line: **"You sell on emotion, but you justify the purchase with logic."** Emotion first, then hand the reader the rational permission slip.
- **Copy length: "long enough to cause the reader to take the action you request."** *"You can never tell the prospect too much about something they're genuinely interested in."* (= Sam's "no such thing as too long, only too boring.")
- **Editing: say exactly what you mean in the fewest words.** Cut "that"s, combine sentences, delete anything that doesn't earn its place, and edit for *rhythm*. The first draft pours out emotionally; the persuasion is built in the edit.
- **Cure beats prevention:** "Selling a cure is a lot easier than selling a preventive — unless the preventive is perceived as a cure." People act to fix a present pain, not to avoid a future one — so frame your offer as the cure.
- **Storytelling:** a story creates "emotional bonding with your prospect." Open with one.

### Two more Sugarman moves
- **Seeds of curiosity:** end a paragraph with a tiny, propulsive line that drags the eye onward — *"But there's more."* "So read on." "Now here comes the good part." "Let me explain." They're cheap and they keep the slide greased.
- **Linking:** introduce the unfamiliar by tying it to something the reader already knows and accepts (relate your new product to a familiar concept, a current event, a feeling they already have).
- **General + specific knowledge:** great copy draws on a wide, curious life (general knowledge) *plus* deep immersion in this product and customer (specific knowledge). Then comes **incubation** — load your brain with the research, step away, and let the concept surface.

### The 31 psychological triggers — a persuasion checklist
Sugarman's triggers are levers to consciously reach for. You won't use all of them in one piece; scan the list and ask "which 3–4 fit *this* sale?"

1. **Involvement / ownership** — get the reader mentally using/holding it ("imagine it in your driveway"; try-it-free). 2. **Honesty** — the most important; readers sense truth, so be candid (it earns everything else). 3. **Integrity** — the whole ad must feel trustworthy and consistent. 4. **Credibility** — pre-empt doubt; specifics and proof. 5. **Value & proof of value** — show *why* it's worth it, don't assert it. 6. **Justify the purchase** — give the logical reason that lets emotion off the hook. 7. **Greed** — a deal too good to pass up. 8. **Authority** — establish you're the expert/leader. 9. **Satisfaction conviction** — a guarantee so strong it removes risk (go *beyond* money-back). 10. **Nature of the product** — match voice to what's being sold. 11. **Nature of the prospect** — know exactly who you're writing to. 12. **Current fads** — ride what's already moving. 13. **Timing** — meet the market when it's ready. 14. **Linking** — tie new to familiar (above). 15. **Consistency** — small yeses → big yeses. 16. **Harmonize / resonance** — keep the reader agreeing (above). 17. **Desire to belong** — buyers join a group/identity (owners of X). 18. **Desire to collect** — sets, series, "complete the collection." 19. **Curiosity** — open loops; "probably the most powerful trigger," especially when the reader can't yet touch the product. 20. **Sense of urgency** — a deadline forces the decision now. 21. **Fear** — of loss/missing out (use ethically). 22. **Instant gratification** — deliver/relieve fast. 23. **Exclusivity / rarity / uniqueness** — scarce, special, not-for-everyone. 24. **Simplicity** — one clear offer, one action; never confuse. 25. **Human relationships** — warmth, a real person behind the copy. 26. **Storytelling** — bond emotionally (above). 27. **Mental engagement** — make the reader do a little work / complete the thought (it sticks). 28. **Guilt / reciprocity** — give something first; people feel a pull to reciprocate. 29. **Specificity** — exact numbers and details read as true ("33%," not "a third"). 30. **Familiarity** — repetition/brand recognition lowers resistance. 31. **Hope** — sell the believable better future.

## Quick checklist before shipping any copy

- [ ] Do I actually have an *idea* worth reading (one that already resonates somewhere), not just a topic?
- [ ] Is it written to ONE specific person, not everyone?
- [ ] Is the headline a sentence I'd say to a friend — and does the sub-headline *continue* it, not repeat it?
- [ ] Have I picked the emotion (awe / anger / curiosity, not sadness) before writing the hook?
- [ ] Does the first sentence punch / open a curiosity gap?
- [ ] Did I spend disproportionate effort on the headline/hook (and the picture)?
- [ ] Does every sentence earn the next (slippery slope)?
- [ ] Is there a story with tension (but/therefore, not and-then)?
- [ ] Customer = hero, brand = guide? Is the *why-I-made-it* story visible?
- [ ] Is the product Trojan-horsed after desire is built (for top-of-funnel)?
- [ ] For a page: does it lead with a *feeling* above the fold, not a feature list?
- [ ] Did I make abstract claims concrete and visual?
- [ ] Are objections pre-empted (8 Mile / anecdotes / FAQ)?
- [ ] Is there proof and social proof?
- [ ] Active voice, no adverbs, no thesaurus words, varied rhythm, ~4th–7th-grade level, sentences starting with And/But?
- [ ] Zero em-dashes and zero semicolons in the final copy?
- [ ] Did I cut a third, then cut a third again?
- [ ] Am I selling a *concept* (not features), on emotion, then justifying with logic?
- [ ] Which 3–4 of Sugarman's triggers am I deliberately using? Is the offer framed as a *cure*?
- [ ] Is it *interesting* the whole way through? (Length is fine. Boring is not.)
- [ ] Is there a plan to actually *distribute* it where the audience already is?
